## I. Blue Light Management

**Dose:** During all screen exposure — 14+ hours/day for heavy users

### Ancient Source

| Source | Text | Reference |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Hippocrates, *Airs, Waters, Places*, ~400 BC | "The light of the sun is the source of health, and its absence is the source of disease. The daily rhythm of light and darkness governs the body's rhythms." | Chapter 2 |
| Avicenna, *Canon of Medicine*, 1025 AD | "The eyes should be protected from strong light and from reading in dim light. Rest the eyes frequently." | Book III, "On the Eye" |

### Modern Evidence

| Study | Finding | Reference |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Czeisler, 2013, *Nature* | Evening light exposure suppresses melatonin by >50%, shifts circadian clock by 3 hours | PMID: 21193540 |
| Chang et al., 2015, *PNAS* | Evening screen use reduces melatonin, impairs next-morning alertness, disrupts REM sleep | PMID: 25535358 |
| WHO/IARC, 2019 | Shift work involving circadian disruption classified as "probably carcinogenic to humans" (Group 2A) | IARC Monograph 124 |

**Evidence Summary:** ★★★★★ (Excellent) — Circadian disruption is a WHO-classified health risk.

### Mechanism

- **Melanopsin (ipRGCs):** Retinal ganglion cells maximally sensitive to 480 nm (blue light) — signal "daytime" to suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)
- **Melatonin suppression:** Blue light after sunset suppresses pineal melatonin production by 50–85%
- **Circadian misalignment:** Disrupts cortisol rhythm, glucose metabolism, immune function, DNA repair cycles
- **Digital eye strain:** Blue light scatters more in the eye, increasing accommodation effort → dryness, fatigue, headache

## II. Sleep Optimization

**Dose:** 7–9 hours per night

### Modern Evidence

| Study | Finding | Reference |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Cappuccio et al., 2010, meta-analysis, *Sleep* | Short sleep (<6h) associated with 48% increased risk of developing/dying from heart disease; 15% increased all-cause mortality | PMID: 21300732 |
| Watson et al., 2015, *Sleep* (National Sleep Foundation) | 7–9 hours recommended for adults; consistent evidence for cognitive, metabolic, immune, and cardiovascular health | PMID: 26039963 |
| Xie et al., 2013, *Science* | Sleep activates the glymphatic system — brain's waste clearance pathway that removes amyloid-beta and tau proteins | PMID: 36093063 |

**Evidence Summary:** ★★★★★ (Excellent) — Sleep deprivation is one of the most well-established health risks.

### Mechanism

- **Glymphatic clearance:** Brain clears metabolic waste (amyloid-beta, tau) — only happens during deep sleep
- **Growth hormone release:** 70% of GH released during deep sleep — tissue repair, muscle growth
- **Memory consolidation:** Hippocampal replay during REM — learning, skill acquisition
- **Immune function:** T-cell activation, cytokine production, vaccine response all impaired by <6h sleep
- **Metabolic regulation:** <6h sleep reduces insulin sensitivity by 30%, increases ghrelin (hunger), decreases leptin (satiety)

### Protocol

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Duration** | 7–9 hours |
| **Consistency** | Same bedtime and wake time daily (±30 min) — the single most important sleep habit |
| **Temperature** | 18–20°C (65–68°F) — core body temperature must drop for sleep onset |
| **Darkness** | Complete blackout curtains; no LED indicators in bedroom |
| **Caffeine cutoff** | No caffeine after 2 PM (half-life is 5–6 hours) |
| **Evening routine** | Red lens glasses 2–3h before bed; no heavy meals 3h before bed; cool shower (triggers temperature drop) |
| **Morning routine** | Outdoor light within 30 min of waking (sets circadian clock for the day) |
