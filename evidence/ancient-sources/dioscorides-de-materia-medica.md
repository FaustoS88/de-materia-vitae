# Dioscorides — De Materia Medica (~60 AD)

> Translated from Beck, L.Y. (2005). *Dioscorides: De Materia Medica*. Georg Olms Verlag.

---

## On Olive Oil

**Book I, Chapter 35**

> "Olive oil, especially from unripe olives, is warming, strengthens the body, and is beneficial for the stomach. It is used in all healing preparations. The oil from ripe olives is less warming but still nutritious."

> "Applied to the skin, it protects against the sun's burning. Taken internally, it loosens the bowels and is good for the throat and chest."

**Relevance:** Olive oil was the universal medicinal base in Greco-Roman pharmacology. Dioscorides recognized both its internal (anti-inflammatory, digestive) and external (sun protection, skin health) uses — consistent with modern understanding of polyphenols and oleocanthal.

---

## On Leafy Greens

**Book II, Chapter 113**

> "Greens are cooling, loosen the bowels, and are nutritious. Chard is gentle and nutritious; beet leaves are cleansing; cabbage strengthens the stomach and is good for the bowels."

**Relevance:** Dioscorides classified greens as "cooling" (anti-inflammatory) and nutritious. Modern science confirms: leafy greens are the most nutrient-dense foods per calorie.

---

## On Garlic

**Book II, Chapter 130**

> "Garlic is heating, loosens the bowels, promotes urination, and expels worms. It is applied externally for bites and internally for coughs and intestinal ailments."

**Relevance:** Dioscorides documented garlic's antiparasitic ("expels worms") properties — confirmed by the 1990s RCT showing garlic extract cleared Giardia in children within 3 days.

---

## On Wormwood

**Book III, Chapter 116**

> "Wormwood (Artemisia) warms, dries, and expels intestinal worms. It is given in wine for intestinal ailments and is good for the stomach and liver."

**Relevance:** Wormwood as an anthelmintic. Modern artemisinin (derived from related *Artemisia annua*) is the standard antimalarial drug. The antiparasitic properties of the Artemisia genus are now pharmaceutical gold.

---

## On Pumpkin Seeds

**Book II, Chapter 130** (under "Seeds")

> "Seeds of gourds and pumpkins are cooling, loosen the bowels, and are gentle on the stomach."

**Relevance:** While Dioscorides didn't specifically document the antiparasitic properties of pumpkin seeds, the broader Greco-Roman and later traditions recognized cucurbit seeds as gentle digestive aids. The antiparasitic action (cucurbitacin paralyzing helminths) was documented more fully in later European folk medicine.

---

## On Nuts

**Book I, Chapter 117**

> "Walnuts are good for the head and are antidotal against poisons. Almonds are nutritious but heavy on the stomach. Pine nuts are warming and strengthening."

**Relevance:** Dioscorides associated walnuts with brain health ("good for the head") — remarkably consistent with modern findings that walnuts (highest in omega-3 ALA and polyphenols among nuts) are most strongly associated with cognitive protection.

---

## On Water Quality

**Book V, Chapter 8**

> "Rain water is the lightest and purest. Spring waters vary in quality according to the minerals they pass through. Water from marshes is heavy and unwholesome."

**Relevance:** Understanding of water quality variation predates germ theory by 2,000 years. Dioscorides correctly identified that water source determines purity — the foundation of modern water filtration.

---

## On Fish

**Book II, Chapter 65**

> "Fish from clean, rocky waters are the most nutritious and easiest to digest. Those from muddy waters are heavier and less wholesome."

**Relevance:** Recognition that fish quality depends on habitat — consistent with modern understanding that wild-caught fish from clean waters have better omega-3 profiles and lower contaminant loads.

## Summary: Dioscorides' Pharmacological Framework

Dioscorides classified substances by their observable effects:

| Quality | Meaning | Modern Equivalent |
|---------|---------|------------------|
| **Warming** | Stimulating, activating, metabolic | Sympathomimetic, thermogenic, metabolic boost |
| **Cooling** | Soothing, anti-inflammatory, calming | Anti-inflammatory, parasympathetic activation |
| **Drying** | Astringent, reducing moisture/secretions | Antimicrobial, astringent, diuretic |
| **Moistening** | Hydrating, soothing, lubricating | Demulcent, emollient, hydrating |
| **Loosening** | Promoting elimination (bowels, urine) | Laxative, diuretic, expectorant |
| **Strengthening** | Tonic, nutritive | Adaptogenic, nutritive, anabolic |

This four-quality system (warming/cooling, drying/moistening) was the pharmacological framework for 1,500 years. Many of his classifications align remarkably well with modern pharmacology — not because he was supernatural, but because careful observation of human responses to substances over decades produces real data.
